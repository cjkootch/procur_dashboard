## 2. Regulatory framework — what changed and why this is different from 2024

The Venezuela sanctions environment has shifted materially since early 2026 in ways that make this opportunity structurally workable where it was not 12 months ago.

Specifically, between January and February 2026, OFAC issued a coordinated set of general licenses substantially expanding authorized activities in the Venezuelan oil and gas sector. The relevant authorizations:

- **GL 46A** — authorizes the purchase, exportation, sale, and refining of already-extracted Venezuelan-origin oil by established US entities (entities organized under US law on or before January 29, 2025).
- **GL 47** (issued February 3, 2026) — authorizes transactions ordinarily incident and necessary to exportation, sale, delivery, transportation of US-origin diluents to Venezuela, including transactions involving PDVSA and PDVSA Entities.
- **GL 48** (issued February 10, 2026) — authorizes transactions ordinarily incident and necessary to provision of goods, technology, software, or services for the exploration, development, or production of oil or gas in Venezuela, including transactions involving PDVSA Entities. **This GL is most directly relevant to the oilfield waste opportunity.**
- **GL 50A** — authorizes certain dispute resolution and contract enforcement transactions.
- **GL 51A** — replaces and supersedes GL 51 to authorize trading of Venezuelan-origin minerals (not just gold) by established US entities.
- **GL 52, GL 54** — further mineral sector authorizations.

Each of these GLs has specific compliance conditions attached — most notably for GL 48: contracts with the Government of Venezuela or PDVSA Entities must specify US law as governing, dispute resolution must occur in the United States, and any payments to blocked persons (excluding local taxes, permits, fees) must go to the Foreign Government Deposit Funds established under EO 14373 of January 9, 2026, or other accounts as instructed by Treasury. There are also reporting requirements to State and Energy Departments within specified timelines after first transactions.

**FAQ 1247 confirms** that non-US persons engaging in Venezuela-related transactions authorized under these GLs do not face US secondary sanctions provided they comply with the GL conditions. Crucially, the GL framework treats non-US persons differently from US entities: non-US persons must be organized under the laws of a third country on or before January 29, 2025 to qualify for the equivalent treatment.

**Vector Antilles' relevance to this framework:** Vector Antilles Management Consultancies FZCO is a UAE entity (Dubai Silicon Oasis), organized in 2024. Provided Vector Antilles meets the temporal threshold (verify with counsel — the brief assumes the entity was established before January 29, 2025; if not, alternate structures may be required), it qualifies as a non-US person able to engage in transactions under GL 48 authority for oilfield services in Venezuela without facing US secondary sanctions, subject to the GL's compliance conditions.

**This is a counsel question that absolutely must be confirmed before any operational engagement.** The brief's regulatory framing assumes Vector Antilles' eligibility; counsel verifies it, identifies the specific compliance requirements that apply, and either clears Vector Antilles for engagement or identifies the corrective structure needed.

## 3. The opportunity profile, distilled

Six elements of what's being offered, separated from the broker's framing into operational specifications:

**Geographic location:** Western Orinoco Belt, south of Estado Anzoátegui. The Orinoco Belt is Venezuela's primary heavy crude region, with active mixta operations under Chevron's expanded GL 46B authority and other Western majors negotiating renewed engagement. The geographic concentration suggests the work is logistically feasible from a single mobilization base, not scattered across multiple disconnected fields.

**Waste volumes:**
- ~300,000 MT of authorized stored open-air pool waste, located 7-15 km from the Management Center, overdue for processing
- ~700,000 MT additional pool waste within 80 km radius
- Subtotal: ~1 million MT of legacy pool waste requiring urgent treatment
- Unquantified but ongoing spill remediation across the production areas — requires mobile units
- Unquantified but ongoing drilling waste from accelerating E&P activity — increasingly subject to permit constraints requiring near-term treatment

**Waste types:** drilling muds, drill cuttings, oily sludge, tank bottoms, contaminated soils. Standard oilfield waste mix; the specific characterization for each pool will determine treatment approach (thermal desorption, biological, solidification, landfarm, etc.).

**Recovery economics:** the broker reports approximately 20% of waste mass is recoverable as crude oil (consistent with industry norms for legacy pool waste at this scale), plus potential mineral and rare earth recovery (less certain; depends on specific source characteristics). Lab analysis and waste classification not yet documented but reportedly available from the Ministry of Environment.

**Commercial structure:** processing fee of $80-100/USD per MT; contractor retains recovered hydrocarbons and other materials. At 100,000 MT/month run rate:
- Service fee revenue: $8M-$10M/month, $96M-$120M/year
- Recovered crude (20% of input mass = 20,000 MT/month): at conservative $40/bbl realized price for heavy Venezuelan crude (after applicable discounts and offtake structure), and ~7.3 bbl/MT, this is ~$5.8M/month, $70M/year additional gross revenue stream
- Combined gross revenue at full run-rate: $166M-$190M/year for the contractor

**Driver:** the *Ley Penal del Ambiente* (Venezuelan environmental criminal law) creates personal criminal liability for operators failing to comply with waste handling obligations. Mixtas are personally exposed; this creates significant commercial pressure on their side to find qualified contractors quickly. **The contractor side has unusual leverage in the commercial negotiation because the buyer side is genuinely time-pressured.**

**Permits:** the broker indicates 100 hectares have been authorized for processing operations and the relevant Ministry contacts have been engaged. Permits are extraordinarily hard to obtain in any oilfield waste jurisdiction; if genuinely in hand, this is a significant standalone asset that adds optionality to the contractor structure.

## 4. The contractor universe — who can actually do this work

The capability requirements are bounded enough that the qualified contractor universe is finite. Three combined capabilities are required:

**(a)** Bulk legacy waste processing capability at 100,000 MT/month sustained run-rate. This is heavy-industrial waste processing — not consulting, not engineering services, but actual physical processing infrastructure that mobilizes to a site, processes 1+ million MT of legacy pool waste over a multi-year timeline, and commissions ongoing operations for new waste streams.

**(b)** Mobile spill remediation capability for the unquantified spill universe across the production areas. Mobile units, rapid-response crews, characterization-and-treatment field equipment.

**(c)** Latin American operating experience — language, regulatory familiarity, equipment mobilization paths, labor practices, supply chain understanding for a region with genuine operational complexity.

The intersection of (a), (b), and (c) is a finite contractor universe. The brief organizes them in three tiers based on capability fit and current Venezuela-engagement posture.

### 4.1 Tier 1 — Global oilfield environmental services majors with Latin American depth

These are the operationally strongest candidates. Qualifying them is a public-information exercise; engagement is gated on their corporate Venezuela posture.

**Veolia / Suez Environment** — the largest global environmental services operator after the 2022 Veolia-Suez merger. Has substantial Latin American presence in Argentina, Brazil, Chile, Mexico, Colombia. The merged entity has both the bulk processing capability and mobile operations capability. **Venezuela posture: needs verification by counsel and direct outreach. The 2025-2026 GL framework changes likely changes their posture vs prior years; pre-2025 corporate policies may have moved toward non-engagement with Venezuela that is now potentially revisable.**

**Clean Harbors (Veolia North America competitor in some markets)** — US-headquartered, primarily North American operations, but has done large-scale legacy waste remediation projects in Latin America historically. Bulk processing capability and mobile fleet are both present. **Venezuela posture: as a US entity, GL 48 framework directly applies; they would need to comply with US-law-governing-contract and Treasury-routed-payment requirements specifically. This makes them a highly relevant candidate but with the most structured compliance path.**

**Newpark Resources** — US-headquartered drilling waste specialists with global oilfield experience. Mature drilling waste processing capability, mobile units. Has Latin American operations including Mexico. **Venezuela posture: same US-entity framework as Clean Harbors.**

**TWMA (Total Waste Management Alliance)** — UK/Aberdeen-headquartered drilling waste specialists. Strong drilling waste processing technology including thermal desorption units (TWMA's RotoMill is industry-recognized). Lower direct Latin American presence than the others but mobilization capability is global. **Venezuela posture: as a UK entity, falls under UK and EU sanctions framework rather than US-direct; counsel needs to verify UK/EU posture given UK retains Russia/Iran-style sanctions on certain Venezuela activities but oil sector posture has evolved.**

**M-I SWACO (Schlumberger / SLB)** — drilling fluids and waste services as a major SLB business line. Q-Sponge, RotoMill-style equipment, global infrastructure. **Venezuela posture: SLB has historical Venezuela operations through PDVSA service contracts; current posture under the post-2026 GL framework requires verification — likely the most directly relevant candidate from the major service company side because of pre-existing operational footprint and infrastructure.**

**Halliburton (subsidiary BJ Services or environmental services arm)** — major US service company with historical Venezuela operations. Similar profile to SLB — pre-existing footprint, current posture requires verification under GL 48. **Highly relevant candidate; their corporate compliance department will have a current view on what is and isn't authorized under GL 48.**

**Baker Hughes** — third major US service company with similar profile. Drilling waste services capability through their integrated service offerings. **Same Venezuela posture pattern as SLB and Halliburton.**

These seven entities (treating Veolia-Suez as one) represent the "global majors" tier. Three are US entities operating directly under GL 48; three are non-US entities operating under FAQ 1247's secondary-sanctions safe harbor; one (TWMA) requires UK/EU sanctions review separately.

### 4.2 Tier 2 — Latin American specialists with mobile and bulk capability

These are operators whose corporate centers of gravity are in Latin America, who often have less restrictive Venezuela engagement policies, and who may be more agile than the global majors for this kind of opportunity.

**Tecpetrol Servicios Ambientales** (Argentina-headquartered, Techint Group subsidiary) — the environmental services arm of Tecpetrol's broader oilfield operations. Mature Latin American operations, mobile capability. Techint Group has operated in Venezuela historically.

**OCEO Brasil / Solví Group** (Brazil) — Brazil's largest environmental services group. Bulk waste processing infrastructure, mobile operations capability, Latin American operating depth. Brazilian foreign-policy posture toward Venezuela is generally less restrictive than US/EU posture.

**Estre Ambiental** (Brazil) — public-company environmental services operator (or its successor entities post-restructuring). Bulk industrial waste processing scale.

**Pluspetrol Servicios** (Argentina) — environmental services arm of Pluspetrol's broader oilfield operations. Has historical Venezuela engagement through Pluspetrol's parent company operations.

**HRT Industrial Services / Petroreconcavo** (Brazil) — smaller scale than the global majors but Latin American specialist with relevant mobile capability.

**Geocycle (LafargeHolcim)** — co-processing specialist using cement kilns for hazardous waste destruction. Has Mexican operations. Co-processing is a specific (and high-margin) treatment approach for certain oilfield waste streams; not appropriate for all the waste types in this opportunity but valuable for specific subsets.

**Auxitech / Servicios de Auxilio Tecnológico** (Mexico, regional Latin American footprint) — smaller environmental services operator with specialized capabilities.

These are 5-7 Tier 2 candidates. The advantages of Tier 2 over Tier 1 are: less restrictive corporate Venezuela policies, faster operational decision-making, often lower cost structures, deeper regional operating experience. The disadvantages are: smaller balance sheets, lower bulk-processing scale, harder to verify capability before engagement.

### 4.3 Tier 3 — Specialist mobile operators and niche capability providers

Smaller operators who would not handle the full opportunity solo but could partner with Tier 1 or Tier 2 contractors as specialist subs. These matter because the opportunity has multiple distinct waste streams (legacy pool, mobile spill, ongoing drilling) that may benefit from specialist subcontracting.

**US Gulf Coast specialist mobile operators** — Texas/Louisiana-based companies focused on mobile spill response and small-volume drilling waste services. Names include Mammoet (heavy lift but adjacent), Cudd Energy Services, Bayou Companies. These would be subcontracted, not prime contractors.

**Trinidad-based oilfield service companies** — Trinidad's mature oilfield service ecosystem includes companies with Caribbean and northern Latin American operating experience. Closer to Venezuela geographically; mobilization is faster.

**Colombian operators with cross-border capability** — Colombia's Casanare and Meta regions are geographically and operationally adjacent to Venezuela; some Colombian operators have informal cross-border relationships and operating experience.

These are subcontractor candidates, not primes. Their identification matters for the operational structuring conversation with the prime contractor, not for the immediate broker-relationship work.

## 6. The hydrocarbon recovery offtake position

This is where Vector Antilles' (and Cole's broader) commodity capability becomes the leverage point in the contractor relationship.

The contractor "gets to sell whatever is recovered" — at 20% recovery on 1 million MT of legacy pool waste plus ongoing recovery, this is approximately 200,000 MT of recovered hydrocarbons over the legacy treatment phase plus ongoing recovery from new waste streams. **This recovered crude needs an offtake buyer.**

Most environmental services contractors do not have commodity offtake infrastructure. They process the waste and recover the hydrocarbons but their commercial relationships are with industrial buyers of the *services*, not with crude oil buyers of the *recovered hydrocarbons*. **This is a structural gap in the contractor's capability that Vector Antilles can fill.**

Three possible offtake structures:

**Structure A — Vector Antilles takes the offtake position directly.** Vector Antilles agrees with the contractor to purchase recovered hydrocarbons FOB at the processing site (or after specified upgrading), at a discount to the relevant marker price reflecting the recovered-crude quality and Venezuela-origin discount. Vector Antilles then onsells through its own buyer relationships. **This is the cleanest structure and the most lucrative for Vector Antilles.**

**Structure B — Vector Antilles brokers the offtake position on commission.** Vector Antilles introduces qualified offtake buyers to the contractor and takes a commission on each cargo lifted. Lower margin than Structure A but lower capital commitment and complexity.

**Structure C — Vector Antilles is silent on offtake.** The brokerage relationship covers only the service contract origination; the contractor handles their own recovered-hydrocarbon offtake. **This forfeits significant value.**

Recommended position: **Structure B at a minimum, Structure A if the contractor's profile is right.** A Tier 1 global major with their own offtake infrastructure may not need Vector Antilles' offtake role; in that case, brokerage commission on the service contract alone is the structure. A Tier 2 Latin American specialist or a Tier 3 partnership consortium will likely need offtake support; in that case, taking the offtake position directly is the value-multiplier for Vector Antilles.

The offtake structure is also **the strongest demonstrable value to the fuel broker** — it's commodity intermediation, which is the language the broker speaks, and it's not what most environmental services intermediaries can offer.

### 7.1 The fuel broker conversation

The primary commercial relationship for Cole is the fuel broker, not the waste opportunity itself. The broker conversation should be structured around demonstrating capability and creating optionality, not around "selling" the waste opportunity solution.

Recommended framing for the broker:

> "I've evaluated the opportunity you mentioned. The framework that makes it work is [GL 48 plus FAQ 1247 secondary sanctions safe harbor for Vector Antilles as non-US person]. The qualified contractor universe is bounded — roughly 7 global majors and 5-7 Latin American specialists. My approach is to do the structured intelligence on each candidate's current posture, identify the right contractor for this specific opportunity, and structure the commercial framework. This framing:
- Demonstrates regulatory sophistication (specific GL framework, compliance conditions)
- Demonstrates operational understanding (qualified contractor universe is bounded, not infinite)
- Demonstrates value-add beyond brokerage (offtake position)
- Doesn't over-promise (no claim to have the relationships already, no claim of guaranteed success)
- Preserves the relationship if the opportunity dies (the broker has seen Cole's analytical capability regardless of outcome)

**The most important success criterion:** the broker should walk away from this conversation thinking "Cole is a serious counterparty I want to work with on the crude deal and on future opportunities." Whether the waste opportunity closes is secondary.

### 7.2 The contractor outreach — gated on counsel clearance

Contractor outreach is operational engagement and is gated on counsel clearance per GL 48 framework. Once counsel clears the engagement structure:

**Phase 1 (Days 1-3 post-clearance):** outreach to 2-3 highest-priority candidates from Tier 1, prioritizing entities whose Venezuela posture has already been publicly verified as engaged or open. Likely SLB or Halliburton given pre-existing footprint, plus Veolia-Suez as the largest pure-play environmental services operator.

**Phase 2 (Days 4-10 post-clearance):** based on Phase 1 responses, either move toward LOI/term sheet with a Phase 1 candidate or expand outreach to Tier 1 remainder + selected Tier 2 candidates.

**Phase 3 (Days 11-30 post-clearance):** structuring of the contractor relationship — service contract framework, offtake structure, engagement with the mixtas through the contractor (not direct).

The outreach voice is Vector Antilles, not VTC. The engagement letter (drafted before outreach begins) makes Vector Antilles' role specific: identification, qualification, commercial structuring, and offtake intermediation.

### 7.3 The mixta engagement — through the contractor, not direct

Vector Antilles does not engage the mixtas directly. The mixtas have their own commercial relationships and procurement processes; introducing Vector Antilles as a separate party between the contractor and the mixta would be operationally awkward and would muddy the value-add structure.

Vector Antilles' relationship is with the contractor; the contractor's relationship is with the mixta. **This entity separation matters both commercially (clean fee structure) and from a sanctions perspective (Vector Antilles' OFAC analysis is bounded by the GL 48 framework, doesn't need to touch the mixta-PDVSA relationship directly).**

The exception: if the broker who brought the opportunity has direct relationships with mixtas, those introductions flow through the broker, not through Vector Antilles independently.

## 10. Risk perimeter and what to walk away from

Even with counsel clearance, certain structural patterns within this opportunity should trigger Vector Antilles walking away:

**Walk-away triggers:**
- Counsel determines GL 48 framework does not cleanly cover the proposed engagement structure
- Discovery that the broker source or upstream parties have undisclosed sanctions exposures
- Mixta or contractor counterparties that include SDN-listed individuals or entities not covered by general license
- Operational structures that require Vector Antilles to take direct PDVSA-side risk (rather than contractor-intermediated risk)
- Any indication that the contracting structure requires payment routing that doesn't comply with the EO 14373 / Foreign Government Deposit Funds framework
- Banking relationships unwilling to clear the proposed payment flows (DIB compliance concerns specifically)
- Any pressure to engage operationally before counsel clearance on specific structures
- Discovery that the "permits" referenced are not what they appear to be (forged, expired, transferable but not held by the broker, etc.)

**These walk-away triggers are documented before engagement begins, not improvised when problems arise.** Discipline matters most when the walk-away cost is highest — exactly when one feels most invested in pushing through.

## 12. Success metrics for this engagement

Three metrics, in order of strategic importance:

**(1) Broker relationship strengthened.** After engagement on the waste opportunity (regardless of outcome), the broker views Cole / Vector Antilles as a sophisticated, reliable counterparty worth bringing additional opportunities to. **This is the primary success criterion** because the broker relationship is the actual commercial asset; the waste opportunity is a single deal.

**(2) Crude deal closes.** The active VTC crude oil deal that is the underlying commercial relationship closes successfully. Success on the waste opportunity engagement strengthens the probability of crude deal closure; failure on the waste opportunity (with grace) doesn't necessarily damage it. The ordering matters: the waste engagement is *for* the crude deal, not vice versa.

**(3) Waste opportunity engagement closes commercially.** Vector Antilles signs an origination/brokerage agreement with a qualified contractor and earns commission and offtake economics on the resulting work. **This is desirable but not required.** Many high-value broker-relationship-building engagements don't close on the originating opportunity but materialize through future deal flow.

If only metric (1) is achieved, the engagement was successful. If (1) and (2), strong success. If all three, exceptional outcome.

If neither (1) nor (2) is achieved — meaning the waste engagement damages the broker relationship and the crude deal also doesn't close — the engagement was a failure regardless of any contractor commercial agreement. This is the failure mode to actively avoid: prioritizing the waste opportunity to the detriment of the underlying relationship.
